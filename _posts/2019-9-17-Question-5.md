# **RULES OF THUMB FOR EFFECTIVE DATA VISUALIZATION**
### *Reiko Okamoto*
---
The year is 2009. The iPhone and Blackberry are starting to replace flip phones and Barack Obama has become the first African-American president. After finishing my homework, I decide to catch up on the latest episode of How I Met Your Mother. As Marshall pulls up a pie chart describing his favourite bars and follows it up with a bar graph describing his favourite pies, I can’t help but be concerned about the [incorrect use](https://www.reddit.com/r/funny/comments/ezrez/this_is_a_pie_chart_describing_my_favorite_bars/) of the pie chart. **It makes absolutely no sense for awesomeness to be distributed across the different bars he frequents!**

<img src="https://i.imgur.com/MiPIl.jpg" alt="https://i.imgur.com/MiPIl.jpg" class="center">

This brings me to the topic of this blog post: **creating effective data visualization**. The essence of data visualization is to transform data into a more digestible format through striking a balance between art and science. In other words, an effective graphical representation should [help your audience make out trends and patterns](https://www.datalabsagency.com/2015/05/21/what-makes-for-great-data-visualization/) that they would’ve overlooked had they only seen the source data.

Sounds daunting? Don’t worry, there are rules of thumb that you can follow. You can compare these rules of thumb to table manners. These guiding principles are easy to follow (see: keep your elbows off the table and don’t chew with your mouth open) and should become second nature to you through experience.

Here are three suggestions to keep in mind when visualizing your data. Let’s dive right into the first rule of thumb. 

### **Identify the message you want to share with your audience**
---
Which party is most likely to win the upcoming federal election? How many cups of coffee do university students drink on average, every day? **What exactly is it that you want to convey to your audience?** Answering this question will not only [give purpose to your end product](https://www.infographicdesignteam.com/blog/5-golden-rules-of-data-visualisation/) but also help you identify the variables of interest.

### **Choose the right tool to visualize your data**
---
When should you use a table? What kind of chart should you use if you’re trying to demonstrate causality? Keep on reading so you don’t make the same mistake Marshall made.

According to [Stephen Few](https://infogram.com/blog/do-you-know-when-to-use-tables-vs-charts/), you should use a table to visualize your data when you’re interested in:
+ Looking up individual values
+ Comparing individual values
+ Dealing with precise values

For example, the table below shows the results from a marathon. You can use this table to see how fast Alice ran the marathon and compare it to Bob’s finish time.

| Bib     | First Name  | Last Name   | Sex | Age | Finish   |
| ------- | ----------- | ----------- | --- | --- | -------- |
| 1001    | Alice       | Smith       |  F  | 25  | 04:25:17 |
| 1002    | Bob         | McDonald    |  M  | 32  | 05:01:43 |
| 1003    | David       | Svensson    |  M  | 37  | 03:53:29 |
| 1004    | Peggy       | Lee         |  F  | 48  | 06:13:56 |

On the other hand, Few recommends using a graph or chart when you want to:
+ Call attention to the shape of the data (e.g. patterns, trends)
+ Compare entire sets of values

It’s also worth mentioning that there’s a [time and place for each chart type](https://www.gooddata.com/blog/5-data-visualization-best-practices-0). 
+ A **line graph** is good for showing how something changes over time. Weight loss progress and [stock price fluctuations](https://www.bbc.com/news/business-46281768) are both examples of data that can be shown using a line graph. The latter is shown below.

+ A **bar graph** is useful when you want to compare the numerical value of discrete categories. [Monthly rainfall in Tokyo](https://weather-and-climate.com/average-monthly-precipitation-Rainfall,Tokyo,Japan) is shown below.

<img src="img/average-rainfall-japan-tokyo.png" alt="https://weather-and-climate.com/average-monthly-precipitation-Rainfall,Tokyo,Japan" class="center" width="500">

+ A **scatter plot** is ideal for showing the relationship between two variables. It’s particularly useful when you want to demonstrate how manipulating one variable affects the other. This type of chart also helps you detect [outliers](https://infogram.com/create/scatter-plot-chart) in your data. Here's a [plot](https://blog.datawrapper.de/introducing-scatter-plot/) showing the relationship between internet speed and GDP.

<img src="img/internet-speed.gif" alt="https://blog.datawrapper.de/introducing-scatter-plot/" class="center" width="500">

+ A **pie chart** is good for showing how parts of a whole are distributed. However, it can be [difficult to take away important information](https://www.act-on.com/blog/data-visualization-101/) when there are too many slices or if the slices are similar in size. If your pie chart looks messy, [create a bar graph instead](https://www.stelayordanova.com/2018/03/pie-chart-dilemma) - your audience will find it easier to compare the height of the bars. Computers typically use a pie chart to display the disk space usage.

Now let's move on to the third rule of thumb.

### **Avoid chartjunk and make use of white space**
---
**[Chartjunk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chartjunk)**, coined by Edward Tufte, refers to unnecessary or confusing elements of the graphical representation that distracts the viewer from the take-home message. Chartjunk includes:
* Thick gridlines
* Busy wallpapers
* Redundant text
* Hard-to-read fonts

Here's a GIF summarizing [Tufte’s philosophy](https://www.darkhorseanalytics.com/blog/data-looks-better-naked) on data visualization. It serves as a reminder to *keep it simple!*

---
So let’s review the rules of thumb: (1) **identify the message you want to share with your audience**; (2) **choose the right visual**; and (3) **avoid chartjunk and make use of white space**.

I hope this blog post served as a helpful introduction to the principles of effective data visualization. Good luck visualizing!
